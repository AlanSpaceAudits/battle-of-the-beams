  4. EQUISIGNAL CROSSOVER ANALYSIS
==============================================================================

  The equisignal corridor is NOT at the beam peak.  The pilot flies at
  the crossover point where the dot and dash sub-beams have equal
  amplitude.  At this crossover, each sub-beam is several dB below its
  own peak.  The deeper the crossover, the narrower the equisignal but
  the weaker the signal.

  The squint angle (angular offset between dot and dash beams) is an
  operator-adjustable parameter, not derivable from the antenna
  dimensions.  The table below shows the trade-off between squint
  angle, equisignal width, and signal level at the crossover.

  Kleve → Spalding (440 km)
  Flat SNR at beam peak:  85.7 dB
  Globe SNR at beam peak: 8.6 dB  (Fock diffraction loss = 77.1 dB)

    Squint   X-over   Flat SNR  Globe SNR   Eq width
     (deg)     (dB)   at eqsig   at eqsig       (yd)
  -------- -------- ---------- ---------- ----------
      1.00     -0.5     85.2 dB      8.2 dB       8712
      1.50     -1.1     84.6 dB      7.5 dB       5642
      2.00     -2.0     83.7 dB      6.7 dB       4052
      2.44     -3.0     82.7 dB      5.6 dB       3151
      3.00     -4.8     81.0 dB      3.9 dB       2334
      3.50     -6.8     78.9 dB      1.8 dB       1775
      4.00     -9.6     76.1 dB     -0.9 dB       1304
      4.50    -13.5     72.2 dB     -4.9 dB        876
      5.00    -20.0     65.7 dB    -11.4 dB        457

  The trade-off: a narrow equisignal (< 1000 yd) requires a deep
  crossover (> 10 dB below peak), which on the globe model pushes the
  individual dot/dash signals below the noise floor.  A shallow
  crossover keeps the signal above noise but gives an equisignal
  several kilometres wide.

  On the flat model, this trade-off does not exist.  With 86 dB
  at the beam peak, even a -19 dB crossover leaves 67 dB of SNR
  at the equisignal.  Any squint angle works.

  On the globe model, no squint angle simultaneously produces a narrow
  equisignal (500 yd) and a usable signal at the crossover.

  6. KEY FINDINGS
==============================================================================

  A. EQUISIGNAL WIDTH
     Measured at Spalding: 400–500 yd (366–457 m)
     Flat-model prediction: 554 yd (506 m)
     → Match within 11%

  B. BEAM VERTICAL STRUCTURE
     On flat surface:  beam arrives at TX elevation ± antenna pattern
     On globe:  beam must diffract over the horizon
       → Creeping wave fills a 48 km vertical ribbon
       → 105× wider than the measured equisignal

  C. SIGNAL AT THE EQUISIGNAL (corrected from beam peak)
     The pilot flies at the equisignal crossover, where each sub-beam
     is ~19 dB below its peak (for the squint that gives 500 yd).

     Kleve → Spalding:
       Flat model:  SNR at equisignal = 67 dB  (usable)
       Globe model: SNR at equisignal = -10 dB  (BELOW NOISE)
       Each dot/dash sample is 10 dB below the noise floor.
       The receiver hears noise, not signal, at the equisignal.

     Stollberg → Beeston:
       Flat model:  SNR at equisignal = 63 dB  (usable)
       Globe model: SNR at equisignal = -87 dB  (BELOW NOISE)

  D. THE SQUINT/SNR TRADE-OFF (Globe model only)
     On a globe, narrow equisignal requires deep crossover, which
     pushes the signal below the noise floor.  Shallow crossover
     keeps marginal signal but widens equisignal to kilometres.
     No squint angle gives both narrow equisignal AND usable signal.
     On a flat surface, this trade-off does not exist (86 dB headroom).

  E. CONTINUOUS BEAM TRACKING
     Aircraft flew INSIDE the beam continuously from Germany to England.
     On a globe, the beam enters the shadow zone after 378 km.
     For Kleve → Spalding (440 km), the last 62 km
     are beyond radio line-of-sight even with aircraft at 6 km altitude.
     For Stollberg → Beeston (694 km), 316 km are
     beyond radio line-of-sight (46% of the total path).

==============================================================================
  CONCLUSION: The measured beam properties (narrow equisignal, sufficient
  signal for precision navigation) are consistent with rectilinear
  propagation.  On a spherical Earth, the Fock diffraction loss combined
  with the equisignal crossover loss places the individual dot/dash
  signals below the noise floor for any squint angle that gives a narrow
  equisignal corridor.
